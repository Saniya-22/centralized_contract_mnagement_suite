QUERY:
Why would a general contractor submit an REA and not a change order to the government?

RESPONSE:
Our system is still expanding its coverage here. Where it's critical, we recommend confirming with your contract or CO.

**Recommended steps:**

1. **Understand the Purpose of an REA:** A Request for Equitable Adjustment (REA) is typically submitted when a contractor believes they are entitled to an adjustment in the contract terms due to changes that affect the cost or time of performance. Unlike a change order, which is a formal modification to the contract, an REA is a request for negotiation and adjustment (DFARS 252.243-7002).

2. **Identify the Basis for the REA:** Determine if the situation involves changes that have increased costs or extended timelines, but where a formal change order has not yet been issued. An REA allows the contractor to seek compensation for these impacts while maintaining the original contract terms until an agreement is reached (DFARS 252.243-7002(a)).

3. **Prepare the REA Documentation:** Ensure that the REA accurately reflects the contract adjustment sought and includes only the costs related to the change. The request should not include costs already reimbursed or separately claimed (DFARS 252.243-7002(a)).

4. **Certify the REA if Required:** If the REA exceeds the simplified acquisition threshold, it must be certified by an authorized individual, confirming that the request is made in good faith and that the supporting data are accurate and complete (DFARS 252.243-7002(b)).

5. **Submit the REA to the Contracting Officer:** Provide the REA to the Contracting Officer for review and negotiation. This step is crucial as it initiates the process of negotiating the equitable adjustment (DFARS 252.243-7002).

6. **Negotiate the Adjustment:** Engage in discussions with the Contracting Officer to reach an agreement on the equitable adjustment. This may result in a formal contract modification if the REA is accepted.

For contract-specific decisions, consult the contract and/or legal counsel.

PREVIOUS RESPONSE:
A general contractor would submit a Request for Equitable Adjustment (REA) rather than a change order because of the following reasons:

1. Initiation Authority:
   - REA: The contractor initiates an REA when they believe they are entitled to an adjustment in the contract price or schedule due to unforeseen circumstances or changes impacting their performance. The contractor does not have the authority to issue a change order.
   - Change Order: Only the government, through the contracting officer, can issue a change order. It is a formal modification to the contract initiated by the government.

2. Circumstances:
   - REA: Used when the contractor encounters unexpected conditions, such as increased costs due to tariffs, delays caused by government actions, or other impacts not covered by the original contract terms.
   - Change Order: Typically used when the government requires changes to the scope of work, specifications, or other contract terms.

3. Purpose:
   - REA: Seeks to adjust the contract terms to fairly compensate the contractor for additional costs or time incurred due to unforeseen events or changes.
   - Change Order: Modifies the contract to reflect changes requested by the government, which may or may not involve additional compensation or time adjustments.

4. Negotiation and Approval:
   - REA: Involves negotiation between the contractor and the contracting officer to agree on the adjustment. The contractor must provide justification and documentation for the request.
   - Change Order: The government may issue a change order unilaterally, but negotiations may occur to determine the impact on cost and schedule.

In summary, a general contractor submits an REA when they seek an adjustment due to unforeseen circumstances or changes affecting their performance, while a change order is a formal contract modification initiated by the government to alter the contract terms.
